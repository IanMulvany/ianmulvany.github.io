Consensus: What Works Well

  • Research-grounded framework Empirical basis from CHI 2025 study (N=155). Goes beyond binary disclosure.
  • Granular dimensions Five well-chosen dimensions covering proportion, contribution type, initiative, review, and model.
  • Familiar patterns Creative Commons-style approach and CRediT-inspired structure fit existing scholarly practice.
  • Interpret feature Readers can paste a statement and understand it - aids adoption and comprehension.
  • Low friction All fields optional, local storage, simple guided flow. Reduces barrier to adoption.

Consensus: Key Gaps & Issues

  • No verification Voluntary self-report only. No checks, no consequences for non-disclosure.
  • No academic stage mapping Cannot report AI use by research stage (lit search, methods, analysis, writing).
  • No machine-readable schema No JSON-LD, no DOI/ORCID integration, no JATS export.
  • Single-artifact assumption Cannot attribute differently per section, figure, code, or supplementary material.
  • No publisher/journal alignment No mapping to Elsevier, Nature, ACM, ICMJE policies or submission systems.
  • Limited reproducibility Free-text model field - no structured version, date, or access mode.

Priority Recommendations

HIGH PRIORITY

  • Stage-based disclosure (map to CRediT/AID)
  • Structured model metadata (name, version, date, access mode)
  • Per-section / per-artifact attribution
  • Machine-readable schema (JSON-LD)
  • Publisher policy mapping (Elsevier, Nature, ACM)
  • Mandatory author affirmation checkbox

MEDIUM PRIORITY

  • Journal-specific presets / templates
  • Multi-author support
  • Export to BibTeX, JATS, LaTeX
  • Output-type selector (text, code, figure)
  • Submission system integration (ScholarOne, Editorial Manager)
  • Accessibility & localization
