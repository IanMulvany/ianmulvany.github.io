3 / 14

Consensus: What Works Well

  • Research-grounded framework Empirical basis from CHI 2025 study (N=155). Goes beyond binary disclosure to structured dimensions.
  • Granular dimensions Five well-chosen dimensions: proportion, contribution type, initiative, review status, and model identification.
  • Familiar design patterns Creative Commons-style approach and CRediT-inspired structure fit existing scholarly practice.
  • Interpret feature Readers can paste a statement and see what it means - aids adoption and cross-audience comprehension.
  • Low friction UX All fields optional, local storage preserves progress, simple guided flow reduces barrier to use.
4 / 14

Consensus: Key Gaps & Issues

  • No verification Voluntary self-report only. No checks, no consequences for non-disclosure.
  • No academic stage mapping Cannot report AI use by research stage (lit search, methods, analysis, writing).
  • No machine-readable schema No JSON-LD, no DOI/ORCID integration, no JATS export for publisher systems.
  • Single-artifact assumption Cannot attribute differently per section, figure, code, or supplementary material.
  • No publisher/journal alignment No mapping to Elsevier, Nature, ACM, ICMJE policies or submission systems.
  • Limited reproducibility Free-text model field - no structured version, date, or access mode fields.

Conceptual Clarity & Usability

Strong / Good

Author

  • The five dimensions are "well chosen and grounded in research."
  • Proportions, AI contributions, initiative, human review, and model identification go "beyond binary AI used / not used."
  • Dual output formats (short/long) support footnotes, acknowledgements, and appendices.
  • Low friction: all fields optional, local storage preserves progress, simple guided flow.

Publisher

  • Granular, research-based framework aligns with findings that "people assign different credit depending on contribution type, amount, and initiative."
  • Creative Commons-style design and CRediT-inspired structure fit existing scholarly practice.
  • Human review dimension (R/Nr) "directly supports publisher expectations that authors verify AI output."

Reader / Academic

  • Informed by empirical work (He et al., CHI 2025, N=155).
  • Reflects that "attribution should vary by contribution type, amount, and initiative."
  • Produces machine-readable statements with both full and abbreviated versions.
  • Open source (Apache 2.0), clear structure, unit and E2E tests.
7 / 14

Academic & Publisher Fit

Weak / Partial

Author

  • No alignment with AID Framework (14 headings) or CRediT (14 contributor roles).
  • Single-work assumption - cannot specify different attribution per section, figure, code, or supplementary.
  • No multi-author support for per-author AI use.
  • No journal-specific templates or presets for AAAI, Nature, ACL etc.

Publisher

  • Does not map cleanly to publisher policy fields: name of AI tool, purpose, extent of oversight.
  • No section-level attribution (Abstract, Methods, Discussion).
  • No handling of AI-generated images - many publishers restrict or prohibit these.
  • No "No AI" verification path.
  • No integration with Editorial Manager or ScholarOne.

Reader / Academic

  • CHI study uses 10 contribution types - toolkit collapses to 3.
  • CHI study has 4 initiative variants - toolkit has 2.
  • "Proportion" is a coarse proxy for the study's "amount" dimension.
  • No domain-specific options (academic vs creative vs professional).
  • No policy mapping to EU AI Act, ACM, Nature etc.
8 / 14

Reproducibility & Verification

Limited / None

Author

  • Model field is free text - no structure for model version (GPT-4o vs GPT-4o-mini), date of use, or API vs web.
  • No suggested citation format for AI tools.
  • No link to data/code availability statements.
  • "Voluntary, honor-system only" - authors can under- or over-report.

Publisher

  • "Voluntary and self-reported" - publishers need mandatory disclosure at submission, verification options, and clear consequences.
  • No persistent identifier for statements.
  • No mechanism to update or correct attribution post-publication.
  • No support for "No AI" claims.

Reader / Academic

  • "Self-report only; no checks against actual AI usage."
  • No guidance on how to assess "how much" AI contributed.
  • No support for multi-step workflows or multiple AI tools.
  • No published user studies on comprehension, accuracy, or adoption.
  • No comparison with other disclosure methods.

Technical Integration & Adoption

Limited / Moderate

Author

  • No export to BibTeX, RIS, JSON.
  • No integration with Overleaf, Word, or LaTeX.
  • No copy-paste templates for acknowledgements or methods sections.
  • Adoption would improve with journal presets and structured export.

Publisher

  • No integration with submission systems (Editorial Manager, ScholarOne).
  • No structured metadata fields in manuscript metadata.
  • No export to JATS.
  • Abbreviations like "AIA PAI Nc Hin R" are not self-explanatory.
  • Adoption easier if publishers and IBM co-develop a "Scholarly Publisher Profile."

Reader / Academic

  • Standalone tool - no integration with editors or authoring tools.
  • No persistence or versioning of statements.
  • "Community engagement is low (3 stars, 0 forks on GitHub)."
  • Feedback via Monday.com form only - no visible evaluation roadmap.
  • Best treated as a "design probe and starting point" not a definitive standard.

What's Missing: Detailed View

Stage-Based Disclosure

Conceptualization, lit search, methodology, data collection, analysis, writing, visualization, interpretation - none covered

Multi-Modal Attribution

No support for text + images + code. No way to distinguish different AI tools in same workflow

Accountability Framework

No "human responsibility" field. No mandatory author affirmation. No link to legal/ethical frameworks

Structured Metadata

No JSON-LD schema. No DOI/ORCID integration. No versioned persistent identifiers for statements

Policy Compliance

No mapping to Elsevier, Nature, ACM, ICMJE, EU AI Act, or California AI Transparency Act requirements

Empirical Validation

No user studies on comprehension or accuracy. No comparison with free-text disclosure. No adoption data

11 / 14

Priority Recommendations

HIGH PRIORITY

  • Stage-based disclosure dimension (integrate with CRediT / AID)
  • Structured model metadata: name, version, date, access mode
  • Per-section / per-artifact attribution blocks
  • Machine-readable schema (JSON-LD) with persistent IDs
  • Publisher policy mapping (Elsevier, Nature, ACM, ICMJE)
  • Mandatory author affirmation of verification and responsibility

MEDIUM PRIORITY

  • Journal-specific presets and templates
  • Multi-author support (per-author attribution)
  • Export to BibTeX, JATS, LaTeX snippets
  • Output-type selector (text, code, figure, table)
  • Submission system integration (ScholarOne, Editorial Manager)
  • Accessibility, localization, and internationalization
